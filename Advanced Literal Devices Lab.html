Literal Devices Flashcards

Browse through different categories of literal devices. Click on any card to see examples.

comparison

Simile

A comparison between two unlike things using 'like' or 'as'.
Her smile was as bright as the sun.
comparison

Metaphor

A direct comparison between two unlike things without using 'like' or 'as'.
Time is a thief.
emphasis

Hyperbole

An exaggerated statement not meant to be taken literally.
I've told you a million times!
figurative

Personification

Giving human characteristics to non-human things.
The wind whispered through the trees.
sound

Alliteration

Repetition of initial consonant sounds in neighboring words.
Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers.
sound

Assonance

Repetition of vowel sounds in neighboring words.
The early bird catches the worm.
sound

Onomatopoeia

Words that imitate sounds.
The buzz of the bees was loud.
figurative

Oxymoron

A combination of contradictory terms.
Deafening silence filled the room.
figurative

Paradox

A statement that contradicts itself but may reveal a truth.
Less is more.
structure

Anaphora

Repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses.
We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds...
structure

Epistrophe

Repetition of a word or phrase at the end of successive clauses.
See no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil.
emphasis

Euphemism

A mild or indirect term substituted for a harsh one.
He passed away instead of 'he died'.
figurative

Irony

Saying the opposite of what is meant, often for humorous effect.
What pleasant weather! (during a storm)
emphasis

Litotes

Understatement using negation of the opposite.
It's not bad (meaning it's good).
figurative

Synecdoche

Using a part to represent the whole.
All hands on deck (meaning all people).
figurative

Metonymy

Using a related term to represent something.
The White House announced (meaning the President).
structure

Antithesis

Juxtaposition of contrasting ideas in balanced phrases.
It was the best of times, it was the worst of times.
structure

Chiasmus

Reversal of grammatical structures in successive phrases.
Ask not what your country can do for you, ask what you can do for your country.
structure

Zeugma

Using a word to modify two other words in different ways.
She broke his car and his heart.
emphasis

Literal Question

A question asked to make a point rather than get an answer.
Isn't it obvious we need change?
figurative

Apostrophe

Addressing an absent person or abstract idea.
Oh, Death, where is thy sting?
figurative

Allusion

An indirect reference to a person, event, or work.
He was a Good Samaritan yesterday.
structure

Anadiplosis

Repetition of the last word of one clause at the beginning of the next.
Fear leads to anger. Anger leads to hate.
structure

Asyndeton

Omission of conjunctions between related clauses.
I came, I saw, I conquered.
structure

Polysyndeton

Use of many conjunctions for emphasis.
We have ships and men and money and stores.
emphasis

Understatement

Presenting something as less important than it is.
It's just a scratch (when referring to a large wound).
sound

Cacophony

Harsh, discordant sounds for effect.
The grating, scraping sound made me cringe.
sound

Euphony

Pleasant, harmonious sounds for effect.
The lulling, lilting melody soothed me.
structure

Parallelism

Using similar grammatical structures for effect.
Like father, like son.
structure

Antimetabole

Repetition of words in reverse order.
Eat to live, not live to eat.
